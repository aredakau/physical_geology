What is a Crystal?

  • A crystal is a solid material with atoms, ions, or molecules arranged in a highly ordered, repeating pattern.
  • The arrangement extends in all three spatial dimensions.
  • Crystals exhibit distinct geometric shapes and properties, such as symmetry and regularity.
  • Examples: Quartz, Diamond.

Unit Cell

  • The unit cell is the smallest repeating structural unit of a crystal lattice.
  • It acts as the âbuilding blockâ of the crystal.
  • When repeated in three dimensions, the unit cell forms the entire crystal structure.

Crystal Lattice

  • A crystal lattice is the 3D framework of points that represent the repeating arrangement of atoms, ions, or molecules in a crystal.
  • The pattern extends through the entire material, forming the structure of the crystal.
  • Governs the physical and chemical properties of the crystal.

Crystal Forms: Faces, Edges, and Corners

  • Faces: Flat surfaces of a crystal.
  • Edges: Lines where two crystal faces meet.
  • Corners: Points where three or more edges meet.
  • Interfacial Angles: The angle formed between two crystal faces.

Crystal Axes

  • Crystal axes are imaginary lines used to describe the symmetry and geometry of a crystal.
  • These axes pass through the center of the crystal and extend in three dimensions.
  • Typically, there are three axes: a, b, and c, which represent the length, width, and height of the crystal.
  • The axes are often labeled with respect to their orientations.

Angles Between Axes

  • The angles between the crystal axes are crucial in defining the geometry of the crystal.
  • Î± (alpha): Angle between axes b and c.
  • Î² (beta): Angle between axes a and c.
  • Î³ (gamma): Angle between axes a and b.
